OPPD generation options

Facing new clean-air rules and pressure to stop burning coal, the Omaha Public Power District is set to make some big decisions about how it produces power in the next two decades.

In considering its future energy portfolio, the OPPD board is looking at five color-coded options that emerged from a stakeholderr feedback process. The board also could choose a hybrid option with elements from each.

Some assumptions    

Costs

Cost estimates represent initial capital costs financed over 20 years. The district declined to provide price information for its growing wind portfolio, citing confidentiality provisions in the power purchase agreements.

An engineering firm tested dry sorbent and activated carbon injection systems at Nebraska City Station unit 1 and North Omaha Station unit 4. Results from the testing at North Omaha unit 4 were correlated with North Omaha unit 5, which has similar geometry and operating conditions.

  • $20 million to install basic emissions controls on Nebraska City Station unit 1
  • $11 million to install basic emissions controls on North Omaha Station units 4 and 5
  • $26.2 million to install basic emissions controls on North Omaha Station units 1-5
  • $420 million to install advanced emissions controls on Nebraska City Station unit 1
  • $18.8 million to refuel North Omaha Station units 1-3 with natural gas
  • $5 million to refuel North Omaha Station units 4-5 with natural gas
  • $80 million to $100 million to install a new high-pressure gas pipeline at North Omaha Station if all five units are converted to natural gas
  • $785 per kilowatt-hour to install a new natural gas simple-cycle combustion unit
  • $120 per megawatt hour for utility-scale solar power (with investment tax credit)
  • Demand-side management: $45.10 per kilowatt-year for demand reduction, $52.72 per megawatt hour for energy efficiency measures (does not include assumed lost revenue)
  • The estimated cost of 100 megawatts of reduced demand is about $3.4 million a year over 20 years
